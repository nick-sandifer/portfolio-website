The Challenge

Wavez was created as a project for CareerFoundry's UX immersion course. Serving as my client, CareerFoundry proposed the need for an app that would allow ocean aficionados to view water, wave, and weather data to increase their safety and fun while at the beach.

PHASE 01

Evaluating the Market

My first order of business was to understand my competitors - how they were being received by users, and what they had to offer them.

After some browsing through the Apple and Google Play stores, it became apparent that while the market was full of Weather and beach related apps, there were very few (if any) clear favorites dominating the field...

Takeaway:

I was facing tons of competitors, with no clear apps dominating the market.

PHASE 01

Investigating Competitors' Designs

After reading hundreds of reviews of different products and exploring over a dozen leading apps, I began to realize that a competitor's success was mostly determined by their ability to pertain to users' skill levels.

Takeaway:

How a competitor catered to their users' varying skill levels determined their success.

PHASE 01

Understanding My Users

Based on the information gathered during my generative user research, I created sets of user personas and journeys to represent the types of individuals in my target audience.

These served as reference points throughout the project, focusing design choices around the users.

Takeaway:

Design personas and flows served as reference points, focusing my design choices around the users.

User Flows

I created user flows to lay the groundwork for the information architecture (IA) of my app. After a few iterations, I realized users could access most features of the app in just a few steps - but to do so risked exposing users to too much info at once...

I turned to apps like Pinterest, and decided to adopt a similar data-card method of displaying information to reduce cognitive load on users.

Takeaway:

I proposed "data-cards" as a way to break down large amounts of information into simpler chunks for users. Inspired by apps like Pinterest and Tinder.

Retrospective Analysis

Final Thoughts

This was my first UX project as a designer, as such there was a lot of learning and professional growth that I experienced throughout the process.

Because this was a project completed for CareerFoundry's UX Immersion course, the final design was never handed off to developers, and I didn't have the opportunity to improve the design through further rounds of user testing. In retrospect, there are certain features and elements I would have liked to test further (such as the clarity of some data tiles on the home screen). Overall, I had a great time working on the project and attempting to solve such complex design challenges.

What went well:

  • The design responded well to the research conducted throughout iterations of the design phase, even when it required massive overhauls.

  • Considering the learning curve, it was completed quickly and with relatively few unforseen issues.

  • It allowed me as a designer to expand out of my comfort zone and experiment with new software and skills.

What didn't go well:

  • The UI design phase took much longer than anticipated.

  • Complications arose when new guidelines were brought to the table, and adjustments had to be made to compensate.

  • The UI phase dominated some of the design for a short period, and had to be brought back on track to alight with user needs and research.

Areas for improvement:

  • Plan for unforseen challenges during the design process and leave ample time in the schedule in case these issues arise.

  • Practice better communication with the team and client to understand the full scope of what is expected.

  • As a UX designer, practice rationalizing all design decisions with research to ensure choices aren't made for aesthetic reasons.
